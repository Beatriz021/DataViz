Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity homes built in North Central Connecticut

by Emma Willins and Myrian Ayala

Last updated on 12/06/2021

for Data Visualization for All
with Prof. Jack Dougherty
Trinity College, Hartford CT, USA



Since 1989, Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity has been building affordable homes in the city of Hartford and outlying areas. To provide context, this nonprofit housing organization was founded on the conviction that every man, woman and child should have a simple, durable place to live in dignity and safety. Therefore, volunteers build, repair and improve homes that low-income people can qualify for (Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity, n.d). The term ‘outlying areas’ refers to the towns that are surrounded but does not include Hartford's city. One challenge the organization continually faces is finding land they can purchase and obtain permission to build affordable homes, since some suburban towns have resisted housing for low-income residents.

Corey Mackey, Tieasha Gayle and Rev. AJ Johnson, in representation of Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity, shared with us that the organization has been working on expanding their reach beyond Hartford, providing their services outside of Connecticut’s capital. However, they do not necessarily have data nor visualization that can help them be on track on its current position toward achieving this goal. Therefore, as students we worked on answering the following question: Where has Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity built homes in North Central Connecticut over time? To do so, we searched the houses’ year built/sold by habitat for each of the 249 addresses provided in the government official property records for the towns located in North Central Connecticut.

Once we collected all the data in a spreadsheet, we made two charts. The first one provides a summary that speaks more directly to Habitat’s expansion in North Central Connecticut over the decade while the second chart showcases Habitat’s homes built in outlying areas organized by location and corresponding decade of construction so the reader can know where exactly Habitat has built more homes outside of the city. Finally, we created three interactive choropleth maps that provide much richer information since it allows any person, including for people who are not from Connecticut, to not only see where the towns are located in the map but also the estimated number of homes built in each town altogether.

The following column bar chart examines the percentages of where Habitat built homes by decade. Although we can observe that the majority of its homes were built in Hartford (light blue) throughout the decades, we can still see that there is an increasing percentage of homes constructed in outlying areas over the decade, which shows Habitat’s commitment toward broadening its work across the state.

Now, to see where exactly Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity has built homes in outlying townsareas outside of Hartford, we decided to create a bar chart which visually illustrates the proportion of Habitat homes among all other the towns. Here the distinctions between outlying towns become clearer. New Britain stands out as the town that Habitat has done the most work in, which makes sense, given New Britain’s geographic proximity to Hartford. Additionally, New Britain has the largest population out of all the towns Habitat provided data for, excluding Hartford. It is reasonable that Habitat would pursue construction in larger towns given their work in more-population-dense areas such as Hartford.


Figure 2: Numbers of homes built by Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity in outlying areas outside Hartford's city per decade. Stacked bar chart created with Datawrapper. Explore the interactive version

For people who are not very familiar with Connecticut’s towns and their locations, we created three interactive choropleth maps that allow any person to see where habitat has built homes over time and the estimated number in each town. For the maps, we chose to use a continuous linear color legend. Hartford is represented by the darker blue while the other lighter blue colors are the towns where Habitat’s has expanded. To have a better understanding, the color is based on the amount of homes built, therefore, the darker it gets, the more homes were built in that particular town. This map was created using Map Shaper and it allowed us to combine Hartford and Tolland Counties which are located in North Central Connecticut and are where Habitat has been focusing on expanding in the last decades. This visualization helps us compare the maps, which allows us to appreciate how Hartford-Area Habitat for Humanity has been spreading in new towns over the decade. Lastly, the color gray in the maps represents the towns where homes have not yet been built by the organization.
